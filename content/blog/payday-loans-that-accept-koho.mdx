If you bank with KOHO, you have probably noticed that **payday loans that accept KOHO** are harder to pin down than you would expect — some lenders deposit to it without a second thought, while others decline the moment they see a fintech account instead of a big-bank one. This guide explains why that happens, which part of the process actually trips up a KOHO account, and how to get funded anyway. Along the way we will be straight with you about what a payday loan really costs and where a cheaper option is hiding in plain sight.

![A smartphone showing a fintech banking app used to find payday loans that accept KOHO in Canada](https://images.pexels.com/photos/887751/pexels-photo-887751.jpeg?auto=compress&cs=tinysrgb&w=1200)

## Quick Answer

Yes — you can find lenders that will deposit to a KOHO account. KOHO gives you a real account number, transit number, direct deposit, and Interac e-Transfer, so the money can physically land there. The friction is not the deposit; it is the *verification* step. Some payday lenders only accept accounts at the major chartered banks, and many rely on an instant bank verification (IBV) tool that does not always connect to KOHO. The fix is to choose a lender that either supports KOHO in its IBV flow or lets you verify income manually with bank statements. Better still, compare a lower-cost installment loan before you commit to payday pricing.

## What KOHO Is (and What It Is Not)

KOHO is a Canadian fintech, not a chartered bank. It offers a reloadable prepaid Mastercard paired with a spending and savings account that has its **own account number and transit number**. You can set up direct deposit for your pay or government benefits, send and receive Interac e-Transfer, and tap to pay anywhere Mastercard is accepted. For day-to-day money movement, it behaves like a bank account.

The important nuance is what sits behind it. KOHO itself is not a bank, so your balance is held with a regulated partner institution rather than insured to KOHO directly. That distinction rarely matters when you are spending — but it is exactly the detail some payday lenders latch onto when they decide whether to fund your account. You can read more about how KOHO describes its own accounts on the official [KOHO website](https://www.koho.ca/).

## Do Payday Loans That Accept KOHO Actually Exist?

Short answer: they do. Because KOHO supplies a genuine account and transit number and can receive both direct deposit and e-Transfer, a lender has two easy ways to pay you — and most short-term lenders fund by e-Transfer anyway. So on the mechanics of *getting the money in*, KOHO is well equipped.

Where "payday loans that accept KOHO" becomes a real search problem is at approval time. A lender's willingness to fund your KOHO account depends less on the deposit rail and more on two upstream checks: whether their policy allows non-bank accounts, and whether their verification technology can read your KOHO transactions. Clear both, and the deposit is routine. Fail either, and you are declined before funding is even discussed.

## Why Some Payday Lenders Reject a KOHO Account

If a lender turns down your KOHO account, it is usually for one of these reasons:

- **Prepaid and fintech exclusions.** Some payday lenders explicitly will not fund prepaid cards or non-bank accounts, treating them as higher risk. KOHO's prepaid Mastercard can get caught by that blanket rule even though its account works like a normal chequing account.
- **"Big bank only" policies.** A minority of lenders restrict funding to the major chartered banks, so a fintech account simply is not on their approved list.
- **IBV connection gaps.** Many lenders confirm income through instant bank verification providers such as Flinks, Plaid, or Inverite. Those tools support most institutions, but coverage for a given fintech is not guaranteed, and a connection that will not complete reads to the lender as "unverifiable."
- **Thin deposit history.** If you only recently started routing income to KOHO, there may not be enough transaction history for an automated check to confirm steady deposits.

None of these are about KOHO being unsafe — they are about lender policy and tooling. That is why the same account gets accepted in one place and declined in another.

## IBV vs. Manual Verification: The KOHO Workaround

The single biggest obstacle is instant bank verification. IBV is the step where you log into your bank through the lender's flow and a provider returns a read-only snapshot of your deposits and balances. It is fast and it is why so many lenders promise "money in minutes" — but it only works if the provider supports your institution.

When IBV cannot connect to KOHO, the workaround is **manual (document) verification**. Instead of a live connection, you supply proof yourself. This is the same path Canadians take when they look for [payday loans with no IBV](/blog/payday-loans-no-ibv-canada), and it is the most reliable route to funding a KOHO account. A lender that offers manual review will typically ask for:

| Verification method | How KOHO fares | What you provide |
|---|---|---|
| Instant Bank Verification (IBV) | Hit or miss — depends on provider coverage | Online login through the lender's flow |
| Manual / document review | Reliable — works with any account | PDF statements, void cheque or account details, pay stubs |
| Direct deposit / e-Transfer funding | Fully supported by KOHO | Your KOHO account and transit numbers |

The trade-off is speed. Manual review is slower because a human reads your documents, so expect hours rather than minutes. But it sidesteps the exact technology gap that causes most KOHO declines.

## How to Get Approved With a KOHO Account

You can improve your odds well before you apply. To make a KOHO account as easy as possible for a lender to say yes to:

- **Download clean PDF statements** from the KOHO app for the last 60 to 90 days. Screenshots are often rejected; a proper statement is not.
- **Highlight your income deposits** so a reviewer can immediately see regular pay or benefits landing in the account.
- **Have your account and transit numbers ready** (found in the KOHO app) for the deposit and any pre-authorized repayment.
- **Ask upfront whether the lender accepts KOHO** and whether they offer manual verification if IBV will not connect. A two-minute question saves a wasted application.
- **Apply earlier in the day**, since manual approvals often stop at a daily cut-off.

Pulling this together has a bonus: the very same documents qualify you for a lower-cost installment loan, which can be deposited to KOHO just as easily.

## What a Payday Loan Actually Costs in Canada

Before you accept any payday loan — KOHO-friendly or not — be clear on the price. Payday lending is regulated **provincially**, and each province caps the cost at roughly **$14 to $17 for every $100 borrowed** (Ontario sits around $15 per $100). That flat fee sounds small, but over a two-week term it translates to an extremely high annualized rate. It is the most expensive mainstream way to borrow in Canada.

By contrast, other consumer loans — installment loans — are governed by the federal criminal interest rate, capped at **35% APR** since January 1, 2025. On the same amount deposited to the same KOHO account, an installment loan almost always costs dramatically less. ## Safer, Cheaper Alternatives Worth Comparing First

Because KOHO can receive a deposit from almost any lender, you are not limited to payday products. Options that usually cost far less include:

- **A personal installment loan**, repaid over months at a capped 35% APR instead of a lump sum on payday. See our overview of [payday loan alternatives](/resources/guides/payday-loan-alternatives) and purpose-built [payday alternative loans](/loans/by-type/payday-alternative-loans).
- **Credit union small-dollar loans**, which often beat payday pricing for members.
- **Cash advance apps**, which advance a slice of earned wages, sometimes for a small flat fee.
- **Asking for more time** — a payment deferral from a biller or a paycheque advance from an employer can remove the need to borrow at all.

Many of those same lenders deposit to fintech accounts like KOHO.

## Red Flags: "Guaranteed Approval" and Advance-Fee Scams

The KOHO angle attracts a particular kind of bad actor, so keep your guard up. Walk away from any lender that:

- Promises **"guaranteed approval, no credit check"** — legitimate lenders always verify *something*.
- Asks for an **upfront fee** to "release" or "insure" your loan. Real lenders deduct fees from the loan proceeds, never before.
- Cannot show you the **full dollar cost** of borrowing in writing before you sign.
- Is not **licensed** in your province, or contacts you only through text or messaging apps.

Our guide on [avoiding loan scams](/resources/guides/avoiding-loan-scams) covers the warning signs in detail. When in doubt, confirm the lender is registered with your province's consumer-protection office before you hand over a single document.

## The Bottom Line

The honest read on **payday loans that accept KOHO** is that the account itself is rarely the problem — it has a real account number, transit number, direct deposit, and e-Transfer, so the money can land there without trouble. The obstacle is verification: some lenders exclude fintech accounts, and instant bank verification does not always connect to KOHO. Choose a lender that supports KOHO or offers manual review with bank statements, and funding becomes straightforward. Then take one more step before you sign — compare a regulated installment loan, which can be deposited to the very same KOHO account, usually for a fraction of a payday loan's cost.
