If you are trying to find out the **maximum payday loan Ontario** law will actually let you borrow, the honest answer surprises most people: there is no flat dollar limit like "$1,500 max." Instead, Ontario caps two different things — how much a lender can advance relative to your pay, and how much they can charge you for it. Get both numbers straight and you will know exactly what a licensed lender can and cannot do, what your loan will really cost, and where a cheaper option is sitting in plain sight. This is general information to help you decide, not financial advice.

## Quick Answer

In Ontario, the maximum payday loan you can be advanced is **50% of your net pay** for that pay period — so if your take-home pay is $2,000, the most a licensed lender can give you is $1,000. Separately, the **maximum cost** is capped at **$15 for every $100 borrowed**. You also get a **two-business-day cooling-off period** to cancel at no cost, protection from rollovers, and tightly capped default fees. Payday loans are still expensive short-term credit, so it is always worth comparing a lower-cost installment loan first.

## The Maximum Payday Loan in Ontario: 50% of Your Net Pay

Ontario does not set a universal ceiling like $1,000 or $1,500. Payday lending here is governed by the province's **Payday Loans Act, 2008**, and the amount rule is tied to your income: a licensed payday lender **cannot advance you more than 50% of your net pay** for the pay period the loan covers.

"Net pay" means your take-home amount after deductions — not your gross salary. The math is simple:

- Net pay of **$1,000** → maximum payday loan of **$500**
- Net pay of **$2,000** → maximum payday loan of **$1,000**
- Net pay of **$3,000** → maximum payday loan of **$1,500**

This is the practical answer to "how much can you borrow with a payday loan in Ontario." A lender who offers you more than half of your net pay is not following the rules, and that is a red flag worth walking away from. The cap exists for a reason: because the entire balance plus fees is due on your next payday, borrowing more than half your cheque can leave you unable to cover rent, groceries, or bills — and pushed straight into another loan.

## The $15-per-$100 Cost Cap (With a Worked Example)

The second limit is on price. In Ontario, a payday lender can charge **no more than $15 for every $100 borrowed**. That fee is the total cost of borrowing — it already includes interest and any lender charges rolled together. Here is what that looks like in real dollars:

| Amount borrowed | Maximum fee ($15 per $100) | Total you repay on payday |
|---|---|---|
| $100 | $15 | $115 |
| $300 | $45 | $345 |
| $500 | $75 | $575 |
| $1,000 | $150 | $1,150 |

The numbers look modest until you factor in the term. A payday loan is usually due in about **two weeks**, so paying $15 to borrow $100 for 14 days works out to an annualized rate of roughly **391%**. That is not a typo — it is the reason regulators, and we, describe payday loans as the most expensive mainstream way to borrow in Canada.

It is worth knowing *why* the rate can legally be this high. Since **January 1, 2025**, Canada's federal criminal interest-rate cap has been **35% APR** on most credit. Payday loans are deliberately carved out of that cap and regulated province by province instead, which is exactly how a $15-per-$100 charge stays legal. An [installment loan](/resources/guides/payday-loan-alternatives), by contrast, has to live under that 35% ceiling — a world apart from payday pricing. You can compare the two directly in our guide to [payday loans versus personal loans](/resources/comparisons/payday-loans-vs-personal-loans), or run any offer through our [loan calculator](/resources/tools/loan-calculator) before you sign.

## Cooling-Off Period and Repayment Rules

Ontario builds in several protections around repayment, and they all favour the borrower.

**A two-business-day cooling-off period.** After you sign, you have **two full business days** to cancel the loan at no cost. You simply return the principal you were advanced — the cash you actually received — and you owe nothing on top. The lender cannot charge a cancellation penalty or keep any fee. If you signed in a rush and had second thoughts, this is your escape hatch.

**No rollovers or back-to-back loans.** A licensed Ontario lender **cannot give you a new payday loan to pay off an existing one**, and cannot roll one loan into another to extend it. This stops the classic debt spiral where fees pile on fees.

**A right to an extended payment plan.** If you take out **three payday loans within 63 days**, you are entitled to repay the third loan through an **extended payment plan** — spreading the balance over multiple pay periods instead of a single lump sum, at no extra cost. It is a legal right, not a favour, so ask for it if you need it.

Together these rules mean a payday loan in Ontario is meant to be a one-time, short-term bridge — never a revolving line you lean on month after month.

## Default Charges: What a Lender Can (and Can't) Add

Falling behind is where costs quietly balloon with some forms of credit, so Ontario caps default charges tightly. If a payment bounces or you cannot repay on time, a licensed payday lender may charge only:

- **A returned-payment fee of up to $2.50** — for a dishonoured cheque or a failed pre-authorized debit. This can be charged **once per loan**, not repeatedly.
- **Default interest of no more than 2.5% per month**, calculated as **simple (non-compounding) interest** on the overdue principal only.

That is the complete list. A lender **cannot** add administration fees, collection surcharges, or extra penalties beyond these. If your statement shows charges you do not recognize, that alone is grounds to question the lender and contact Consumer Protection Ontario. Knowing these caps also helps you spot a lender bending the rules before it costs you.

## How to Check a Payday Lender's Licence in Ontario

Every business making payday loans in Ontario — storefront or online — must hold a **licence under the Payday Loans Act, 2008** and must clearly display its rates and the total cost of borrowing before you sign. An unlicensed lender is operating illegally, and you have far less protection if something goes wrong.

Before you borrow, take two minutes to:

- **Confirm the lender is licensed.** Check through [Consumer Protection Ontario](https://www.ontario.ca/page/consumer-protection-ontario), the provincial body that regulates payday lending and can verify a licence.
- **Read the cost box.** The lender must show the total dollar cost — not just a rate — in writing. If they will not, walk away.
- **Reject "guaranteed approval, no checks."** No legitimate lender can guarantee approval before verifying your income and identity. That phrase is the single most common lure in [loan scams](/resources/guides/avoiding-loan-scams).
- **Never pay a fee upfront.** Real lenders deduct their fee from your loan proceeds; they never ask you to send money first to "release" funds.

You can read the actual law for yourself in Ontario's [Payday Loans Act, 2008](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/08p09), and see the federal perspective on costs and rights through the [Financial Consumer Agency of Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/financial-consumer-agency/services/loans/payday-loans.html). ## The Bottom Line

The **maximum payday loan Ontario** rules allow is defined by two caps, not one dollar figure: a lender can advance you **no more than 50% of your net pay**, and can charge **no more than $15 per $100 borrowed**. On top of that you get a two-business-day cooling-off period, protection from rollovers, a right to an extended payment plan after three loans in 63 days, and strictly capped default fees. Those rules exist because a payday loan is expensive, short-term credit that is easy to fall behind on. Use them: confirm the lender is licensed, read the full cost in dollars, never pay a fee upfront, and — before you borrow at 391% — check whether a regulated installment loan at 35% would solve the same problem for far less.
